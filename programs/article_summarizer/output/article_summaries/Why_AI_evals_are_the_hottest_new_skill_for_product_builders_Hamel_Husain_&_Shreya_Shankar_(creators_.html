Why AI Evals Are the Hottest New Skill for Product Builders

Hamel Husain and Shreya Shankar, creators of the world's most popular AI evals course, reveal why systematic evaluation has become the most critical skill for building successful AI products. Two years ago, few had heard of 'evals' - now it's essential for any serious AI product builder.

What Are AI Evals? (5:51)

AI evals are systematic ways to measure and improve AI applications. At its core, it's data analytics applied to LLM applications in a structured way to identify problems, create metrics, and iterate improvements. Think of it as moving beyond 'vibe checks' to quantifiable measurement systems that give you confidence in your AI product's performance.

The Process: Error Analysis First (16:51)

The most crucial step is manual error analysis - examining real user traces to understand where your AI fails. Using a property management AI assistant as an example, Husain demonstrates how to review conversation logs, write notes on problems, and identify patterns. This human-driven analysis cannot be automated with LLMs initially, as they lack the product context to identify subtle failures.

Open Coding and Axial Analysis (31:39)

After collecting error notes, teams use 'open coding' (informal note-taking) followed by 'axial coding' (categorization) to organize problems. LLMs excel at this synthesis step, helping transform messy notes into actionable categories like 'human handoff issues' or 'conversational flow problems.' A 'benevolent dictator' - typically the domain expert - should lead this process to avoid committee paralysis.

Building LLM-as-Judge Systems (46:06)

For complex failure modes that can't be caught with simple code, teams build 'LLM-as-judge' evaluators. These focused AI systems evaluate one specific failure mode with binary pass/fail outputs. The key is making them highly specific and testing them against human judgment to ensure alignment before deployment.

Evals as the New PRDs (60:51)

Well-crafted eval prompts effectively become living product requirements documents. They specify exactly how the AI should behave in specific scenarios, running continuously to catch regressions. This represents a fundamental shift in how product teams define and maintain AI product quality.

The Great Evals Debate (70:00)

Despite some high-profile claims that companies like Claude Code operate on 'vibes only,' Shankar argues this misses the broader picture. Even companies claiming to avoid evals are doing systematic error analysis and quality measurement - they're just not calling it 'evals.' The debate often stems from narrow definitions and past bad experiences with poorly implemented evaluation systems.

The time investment is manageable: 3-4 days for initial setup, then just 30 minutes per week for ongoing monitoring. For most products, teams need only 4-7 LLM-as-judge evaluators to cover their critical failure modes. This systematic approach transforms AI product development from guesswork into measurable, improvable processes.
