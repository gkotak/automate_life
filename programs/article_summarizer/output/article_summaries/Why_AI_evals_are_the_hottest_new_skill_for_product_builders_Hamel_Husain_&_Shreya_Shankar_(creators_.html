Summary

AI evals (evaluations) have emerged as the most critical new skill for product builders working with AI applications. According to Hamel Husain and Shreya Shankar, creators of the world's most popular AI evals course, evals are systematic ways to measure and improve AI applications through data analysis and automated testing. This isn't about creating perfect evaluation systems, but rather building actionable feedback loops that help improve AI products systematically.

The core methodology involves a structured three-step process: error analysis (manually reviewing real user traces to identify failure patterns), categorization (using AI to group similar issues into 'axial codes'), and building automated evaluators (creating LLM-as-judge systems to catch these issues at scale). This approach is grounded in traditional data science techniques like open coding and theoretical saturation, adapted specifically for the stochastic nature of AI systems.

The speakers demonstrate this process using real examples from a property management AI assistant, showing how manual trace review reveals unexpected issues like poor handoff protocols and conversational flow problems. Rather than relying on generic benchmarks, successful AI products need domain-specific evals that reflect actual user interactions and business requirements. The process typically requires 3-4 days of initial setup but then only 30 minutes weekly maintenance, making it highly scalable for improving AI product quality.

Despite recent controversy in the AI community about whether evals are necessary (sparked by claims that some successful products rely only on 'vibes'), Husain and Shankar argue that all successful AI applications use some form of systematic evaluation, even if not formally labeled as 'evals.' They position evals as the new PRDs (Product Requirements Documents) for AI products - living specifications that continuously test whether AI systems meet business and user needs.
