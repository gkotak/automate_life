Summary

China's new export controls on rare earth metals represent a sweeping escalation in the U.S.-China trade war, with Beijing imposing restrictions that could give it effective veto power over nearly all advanced technology, including AI. The regulations, taking effect in November and December 2025, extend beyond previous limits to include electric motors, computer chips, and other critical components manufactured primarily in China. While initially broader, the restrictions have been narrowed to focus explicitly on military applications after Trump threatened 100% retaliatory tariffs.

Ben Thompson draws a crucial parallel between China's rare earth dominance and the U.S.'s chip controls, but highlights a fundamental difference: while the U.S. leads in semiconductors because it created the industry, America trails in rare earths due to self-inflicted wounds. Starting in 1980, the Nuclear Regulatory Commission reclassified rare earth mining waste as a nuclear hazard, dramatically increasing costs and choking an industry the U.S. had dominated. Meanwhile, China strategically flooded the market with cheap rare earths, leading to the closure of most U.S. facilities.

The situation deteriorated further when American magnet manufacturer Magnequench was sold to Chinese interests in 1995, with all equipment and expertise moving to China exactly five years later as stipulated by regulators. This resulted in the U.S. going from total rare earth dominance in 1980 to essentially zero presence across the entire supply chain within just 20 years—a completely avoidable strategic disaster.

Thompson argues that while China must figure out how to build future technology, the U.S. faces the arguably harder challenge of rebuilding capabilities it once possessed. The core problem isn't technological—America has abundant rare earth deposits and the know-how exists—but rather political will. The U.S. struggles to apply cost-benefit analysis to regulations, implement targeted industrial policies, or accept higher input costs for strategic independence, making China's regulatory and bureaucratic paralysis its greatest geopolitical asset in this competition.
