Summary

China has imposed sweeping export controls on rare earth metals and related technologies, taking effect in November and December 2025, giving Beijing significant leverage over global technology supply chains. The restrictions initially covered a broad range of applications but were later narrowed to focus explicitly on military uses after President Trump threatened 100% retaliatory tariffs. This represents China's most powerful economic weapon in the ongoing tech trade war, as the country dominates over 90% of global rare earth production and processing.

Ben Thompson draws stark parallels between this situation and previous U.S. chip controls on China, noting a critical difference: America leads in semiconductors because it created the industry, while it trails in rare earths due to self-inflicted regulatory wounds. Starting in 1980, the Nuclear Regulatory Commission reclassified rare earth mining waste as nuclear hazard, dramatically increasing costs. Meanwhile, China strategically flooded the market with cheap rare earths, leading to the closure of U.S. facilities. The final blow came in 1995 when Magnequench, America's key magnet manufacturer, was sold to China and relocated after the required five-year U.S. production period.

The fundamental challenge facing America is not technological catch-up but political will. The U.S. went from total rare earth dominance in 1980 to zero presence in just 20 years through regulatory overreach and strategic neglect. Thompson argues that America's biggest disadvantage is its inability to build, remove burdensome regulations, or accept higher input costs for strategic independence. While the U.S. has abundant rare earth deposits and China's mining share has dropped to 70%, the real chokepoint remains processing capacity, which requires overcoming regulatory obstacles and implementing protective tariffs against Chinese dumping.
