Sora the App, Sonnet 4.5 and the Question of Models as Processors

OpenAI's Sora App Launch: A New Social Video Platform

OpenAI launched Sora 2, an updated AI video generator alongside a new social video app for iPhone. The invite-only app resembles TikTok but focuses on AI-generated content, allowing users to create deepfakes of friends with consent. Currently limited to US and Canada users, the app represents OpenAI's attempt to create what employees call the "ChatGPT moment for video generation."

The 90/9/1 Rule in AI Video Competition

The AI video landscape reveals three distinct approaches targeting different user segments. YouTube focuses on the 1% of creators with tools for content production. OpenAI targets the 9% of editors and distributors with easy-to-use creation tools. Meta's Vibes app targets the 90% of consumers who primarily consume content rather than create it.

Thompson argues that Meta's approach is most compelling for the majority of users, as the Sora app feels more like a "parlor trick" that loses appeal quickly. The key challenge for OpenAI is that while creation is a prerequisite to consumption, it's not the main driver of engagement. Video creation requires others to consume it, and AI-generated content may not be inherently interesting enough to sustain long-term engagement.

Anthropic's Claude Sonnet 4.5: Advanced Coding Capabilities

Anthropic released Claude Sonnet 4.5, designed specifically for extended coding tasks. The model can code autonomously for up to 30 hours straight, compared to 7 hours for the previous Claude Opus 4. The release demonstrates Anthropic's focus on developer tools and AI agents that handle complex coding tasks.

Significantly, Cognition had to completely rebuild their Devin AI coding assistant for Sonnet 4.5, rather than simply swapping in the new model. This rebuilding process revealed fundamental changes in how the model operates, requiring new architectural approaches to harness its capabilities effectively.

Models as Processors: The Evolution Question

Thompson explores whether AI model evolution will mirror the processor evolution of the 1980s and 1990s. During that era, software developers could write to the edge of current processor capabilities, confident that future processors would be faster but functionally compatible, allowing applications to benefit from performance gains without major changes.

Microsoft CTO Kevin Scott previously compared AI models to the x86 platform evolution, suggesting a similar pattern of exponential capability increases while maintaining compatibility. However, the Devin rebuild example suggests AI models may evolve in more fundamental ways that break backward compatibility and require architectural rethinking.

Strategic Implications and Market Positioning

The analysis reveals how each company's AI video strategy aligns with their core business models and historical strengths. YouTube leverages its creator economy, OpenAI pushes active AI usage, and Meta focuses on passive consumption‚Äîthe area where it has historically excelled. This alignment suggests that successful AI applications may need to match both technological capabilities and company DNA.

The broader question of model evolution has significant implications for AI application development. If models evolve like processors with backward compatibility, developers can build for current capabilities. If they evolve more fundamentally, requiring architectural changes, the development approach becomes more complex and uncertain.
