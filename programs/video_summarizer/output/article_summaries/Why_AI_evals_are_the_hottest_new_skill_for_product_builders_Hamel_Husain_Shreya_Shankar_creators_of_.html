Why AI Evals Are the Hottest New Skill for Product Builders

This comprehensive discussion with Hamel Husain and Shreya Shankar, creators of the #1 AI evals course on Maven, explores why evaluations have become the most critical new skill for AI product builders.

üéØ The Rise of Evals as a Core Skill ‚è∞ 1:09

Evals have emerged from obscurity to become one of the most necessary skills for AI product builders. Both CPOs of Anthropic and OpenAI have identified evals as the most important new skill for product builders. This represents a rare emergence of an entirely new skill category that product teams must master to be successful.

üîç What Are Evals? ‚è∞ 5:28

At its core, evals are a systematic way to measure and improve AI applications. They represent data analytics applied to LLM applications, providing structured methods for understanding performance and enabling iteration and experimentation. The process involves creating metrics around AI outputs to measure what's happening and drive improvements.

‚ö° High ROI Activity ‚è∞ 0:00

Building evals is described as the highest ROI activity teams can engage in when developing AI products. The process is addictive once teams start, as it provides immediate learning and insights. Importantly, for most products, this process only needs to be done once and then built upon.

üéØ Goal-Oriented Approach ‚è∞ 0:18

The fundamental principle is that the goal isn't to do evals perfectly, but to actionably improve your product. This practical focus helps teams avoid perfectionism and concentrate on driving real improvements.

üö´ Common Misconceptions ‚è∞ 0:23

There's significant controversy and strong opinions around evals, often from teams who have been burned by poorly implemented evaluation processes. The most common misconception is believing that AI can automatically evaluate itself - this approach simply doesn't work in practice.

üëë The Benevolent Dictator Model ‚è∞ 0:48

Teams should avoid committee-based approaches that bog down the eval process. Instead, appointing one person with domain expertise (often the product manager) as a "benevolent dictator" keeps the process efficient and decisive. This prevents the process from becoming too expensive to execute regularly.

üìö Practical Implementation

The article emphasizes several key practices: starting with manual error analysis before automation, using "open coding" and "axial coding" methodologies, treating evals as the new PRDs for AI products, and maintaining a regular cadence of around 30 minutes per week for eval maintenance.
